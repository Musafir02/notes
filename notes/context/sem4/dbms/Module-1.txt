This page delves into the concept of three-tier and four-tier architectures in Database Management Systems (DBMS), highlighting their advantages and disadvantages.

**Three-Tier Architecture**

The three-tier architecture is the most popular client-server architecture in DBMS, comprising three layers:

*   **Presentation Layer (User Interface)**: Interacts with the user through a user-friendly interface.
*   **Application Layer (Business Logic)**: Handles the business logic and processing of data.
*   **Data Layer (Database)**: Stores and manages the data.

The benefits of this architecture include:

*   **Flexibility and Scalability**: Each layer can be modified or upgraded independently without affecting the others.
*   **Improved Security**: The database is isolated from the other layers, enhancing security.

However, it also has some drawbacks:

*   **Complexity**: The additional layer increases complexity and maintenance costs.
*   **Cost**: The additional layer requires skilled personnel for development and maintenance.

**Four-Tier Architecture**

The four-tier architecture extends the three-tier model by adding an additional layer for external integration or specialized services like authentication, caching, or API management.

**Advantages and Disadvantages**

The four-tier architecture offers:

*   **Improved Functionality**: Divides functionality into distinct layers for better organization.
*   **Additional Layer**: Handles services such as web services or middleware integration.

However, it also has some drawbacks:

*   **Additional Complexity**: The additional layer increases complexity and maintenance costs.
*   **Cost**: The additional layer requires skilled personnel for development and maintenance.

**Example**

A cloud-based application like Salesforce is an example of a four-tier architecture, with a user interface via web or mobile, application layer handling business logic and processes requests, and a presentation layer interacting with the user.

**Key Takeaways**

In conclusion, the three-tier and four-tier architectures are two common approaches to designing DBMS systems. While the three-tier architecture is the most popular, the four-tier architecture offers improved functionality and additional services. However, both architectures have their advantages and disadvantages, and the choice of which one to use depends on the specific needs of the project.

The author begins by defining these essential properties and explaining their significance in ensuring reliable database transactions.

**Atomicity**

Atomicity is the property that ensures a transaction is executed entirely or not at all. It implies that the operation should not be performed partially or in different segments. For instance, when transferring money from one account to another, the entire transaction should be completed successfully or not at all. If the transaction fails, the operation should not leave the accounts in an inconsistent state.

**Consistency**

Consistency refers to the property that the database must remain in a valid state before and after a transaction. This means that the integrity of the data should always be maintained. For example, if a transaction is executed in the database, its changes should be preserved further in the database. In the case of transactions, data integrity is essential to maintaining the consistency of data.

**Isolation**

Isolation is the property that ensures that the operations of multiple transactions do not affect each other. It means that transactions do not affect each other, even if they are executed concurrently. For example, when Ninja has an account A with ₹300 in his account, Ninja wishes to send ₹100 to Pranay's account, that is, account B. In account B, Pranay already has a balance of ₹1000.

**Durability**

Durability is the property that ensures that the changes made by a transaction are permanent after its commit. It means that the changes made by a transaction are permanent and cannot be rolled back.
