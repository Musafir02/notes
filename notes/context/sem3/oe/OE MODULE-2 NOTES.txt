1. The Eight Directions and Their Names 
The eight primary directions are an extension of the four cardinal points. They are equally spaced 
at 45° angles from each other. 
• 
Cardinal Directions: North (N), East (E), South (S), West (W). 
• 
Ordinal (Intercardinal) Directions: Northeast (NE), Southeast (SE), Southwest (SW), Northwest 
(NW). 
                                           
 
 
The ordinals sit exactly between them: 
• 
NE is between N and E (45°) 
• 
SE is between S and E (135°) 
• 
SW is between S and W (225°) 
• 
NW is between N and W (315°) 
2. Angles Between Directions 
Understanding the angular relationship is crucial for solving problems. 
• 
Angle between adjacent directions (e.g., N and NE, NE and E) = 45° 
• 
Angle between directions with one direction between them (e.g., N and E, E and S) = 90° 
• 
Angle between opposite directions (e.g., N and S, E and W, NE and SW) = 180° 
3. Pythagorean Triplets 
These are sets of three positive integers (a, b, c) that satisfy the equation a² + b² = c², where c is the 
hypotenuse (longest side) of a right-angled triangle. 
Common Triplets: 
• 
(3, 4, 5) → 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5² 
• 
(6, 8, 10) → (3,4,5) × 2 
• 
(9, 12, 15) → (3,4,5) × 3 
• 
(5, 12, 13) → 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13² 
• 
(10, 24, 26) → (5,12,13) × 2 
• 
(7, 24, 25) → 7² + 24² = 49 + 576 = 625 = 25² 
• 
(8, 15, 17) → 8² + 15² = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17² 

Application: If a problem gives you two perpendicular distances (e.g., "3 km North and 4 km East"), the 
shortest distance (straight line) will be the third number in the triplet, 5 km. 
4. 45-45-90 Triangle (Isosceles Right Triangle) 
This is a special right triangle where the two legs are of equal length, and the angles are 45°, 45°, and 90°. 
Ratio of Sides: The sides are in the ratio 1 : 1 : √2 
• 
Leg : Leg : Hypotenuse = 1 : 1 : √2 
Application: This appears frequently in direction problems involving North-East, South-East, South-West, 
or North-West moves. For example, if a person walks 5 km North-East: 
• 
The North component = 5 / √2 km ≈ 5 × 0.707 = 3.535 km 
• 
The East component is the same, 5 / √2 km. 
• 
Conversely, if someone walks 3 km North and then 3 km East, the straight-line distance from the 
start is 3√2 km.

Coding and Decoding 
This reasoning topic tests your ability to understand hidden patterns or logic behind codes and then 
decode/encode accordingly. 
1. Alphabet Coding 
Letters are replaced or rearranged by following a rule. 
Example: 
1) If CAT → DBU, then what is DOG → ? 
• 
Rule: Each letter shifted by +1 in alphabet. 
• 
CAT → C(+1)=D, A(+1)=B, T(+1)=U → DBU 
• 
DOG → D(+1)=E, O(+1)=P, G(+1)=H → EPH 
 
Backward Shift (–n) 
Each letter is moved backward in the alphabet. 
Example: 
2) If MANGO → KYLEM, then how is APPLE → ? 

• 
Rule: –2 shift (M→K, A→Y, N→L, G→E, O→M). 
• 
APPLE → YNNJC   
 
Opposite Letters (A↔Z, B↔Y, etc.) 
Each letter is replaced with its opposite in the alphabet. 
3) Example: 
If BOY → YLB, then how is CAT → ? 
• 
Rule: A↔Z, B↔Y, C↔X, etc. 
• 
BOY → YLB. 
• 
CAT → XZG   
Position-Based Coding 
Code is based on the letter’s position in the alphabet (A=1, B=2, … Z=26). 
4) Example: 
If BAD → 214, then how is ACE → ? 
• 
B=2, A=1, D=4. 
• 
ACE = 1 3 5 → 135   
5) Values Coding 
Each letter is assigned a fixed numerical value (often based on position or a hidden rule). 
Example: 
If PAPER → 57, and P=16, A=1, E=5, R=18, then: 
• 
PAPER = 16+1+16+5+18 = 56 (sometimes +1 adjustment → 57).

2nd letter +1 (E→F),

3rd letter +3 (L→M),

What is Data Sufficiency? 

Data Sufficiency means checking whether the given information (data) is enough to answer a question — 
not actually solving it. 
Data Sufficiency is to check and test the given set of information, whether it is enough to answer a question or 
not. Data Sufficiency-type questions are designed to test the candidate’s ability to relate given information to 
reach a conclusion. Moreover, the data sufficiency has wider attributes to test the candidate. On one hand, it 
may have problems from any of the topics of reasoning or quantitative aptitude and on the other hand, it can 
test a candidate’s analytical skills.  
 
Q1. What is the area of a triangle? 
Statements: 
1. The base of the triangle is 10 cm. 
2. The height of the triangle is 5 cm. 
Options: 
A. Statement 1 alone is sufficient 
B. Statement 2 alone is sufficient 
C. Both 1 and 2 together are sufficient 
D. Even both together are not sufficient 
  Answer: C 
Explanation: Area = ½ × base × height. We need both base and height. 
Q2. What is the perimeter of a rectangle? 
Statements: 
1. The length is twice the breadth. 
2. The area of the rectangle is 72 sq. cm. 
Options: 
A. 1 alone sufficient 
B. 2 alone sufficient 
C. Both 1 and 2 together sufficient 
D. Even both together not sufficient 
 Answer: C 
Explanation: 
From (1): L = 2B 
From (2): L × B = 72 → (2B × B = 72 → B² = 36 → B = 6, L = 12) 
Then perimeter = 2(L + B) = 36.
