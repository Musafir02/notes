A transformer is a static device that can transfer electrical energy from one circuit to another without any change in frequency. It can increase or decrease the voltage but with a corresponding decrease or increase in current. It works on the principle of mutual induction.

A transformer mainly consists of an iron core and two inductive coils or windings placed on a common core. One winding is connected to the source of the electrical energy (called primary winding), while the other is connected to the load (called secondary winding). These windings are of different numbers of turns, wound around the two opposite limbs of the core. Each winding is properly insulated from each other as well as the core.

A core is rectangular in shape and is laminated. The core is constructed of stacks of laminated silicon steel to provide a continuous magnetic path to carry alternating flux.

The primary windings have N1 turns, and the secondary windings have N2 turns. The primary winding is connected to the AC source, and the secondary winding is connected to the load. The transformer works by transferring energy from the primary winding to the secondary winding through the magnetic field created by the primary winding.
